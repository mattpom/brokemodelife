How to Start Investing When You're Broke

You don't need $1,000. You need $5 and the right account. Here are the exact steps to get your first investment going this week — no matter how thin your wallet is right now.

MoneyInvesting · Beginner · Action Plan7 min read

The biggest lie in personal finance is that investing is for people who already have money. It's not. It's for people who understand compound interest — which is the single most powerful force available to anyone with a low income and a long time horizon. The earlier you start, the less money you need to get to a significant number. A 25-year-old investing $50 per month reaches the same place at 65 as a 35-year-old investing $150 per month. That's the math. Starting broke and starting now beats waiting until you're ready.

Here's the actual playbook.

"The best time to invest was yesterday. The second best time is today with $5."

Step 1: Kill the High-Interest Debt First

Before you invest a dollar, eliminate any debt charging more than 8% interest. Credit card debt at 22% APR is a guaranteed -22% return on every dollar you don't pay off. No investment reliably beats that. Pay the minimum on everything, then throw every spare dollar at the highest-rate balance until it's gone. This is not optional. It is the prerequisite.

Student loans below 6%, car payments, and mortgages can coexist with investing. High-interest consumer debt cannot. The math doesn't work in your favor while it exists.

Step 2: Open the Right Account in 15 Minutes

Open a Roth IRA. Not a brokerage account, not a savings account — a Roth IRA. The reason: money grows tax-free inside a Roth, and you contribute after-tax dollars. When you withdraw in retirement, you pay zero taxes on the gains. At a broke income level, you're in a low tax bracket now; you want to pay taxes now and never again. The contribution limit in 2026 is $7,000 per year. You don't have to hit it. You just have to start.

Fidelity, Vanguard, and Schwab all offer Roth IRAs with zero account minimums and zero trading fees. Fidelity has a $1 minimum on their index funds. Schwab has a $5 minimum. Open the account, verify your identity, and link your bank account. It takes 15 minutes.

Where to Open Your Account — The Short List

Fidelity: $1 minimum, best beginner UX, ZERO expense ratio on their index funds (FZROX). Schwab: $5 minimum, excellent tools, good customer service. Vanguard: higher minimum ($1,000 for some funds) but created the index fund concept — worth it if you can get there. All three are legitimate and safe. Don't overthink it. Open one today.

Step 3: Buy One Thing and Stop

When you have $5, $50, or $500 in your new Roth IRA, buy a total market index fund and do nothing else. On Fidelity, that's FZROX (zero expense ratio, $1 minimum). On Schwab, SWTSX. On Vanguard, VTI or VTSAX. These funds own a tiny slice of every company in the US stock market. When the market goes up, you go up. When it goes down, you stay in. Time in the market beats timing the market — always and without exception.

Do not buy individual stocks. Do not buy crypto with money you need to grow. Do not day trade. Buy the total market index fund, set up a recurring $25 or $50 automatic investment, and check it twice a year. That is the entire strategy. Anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something.

The $5 to Start Now Plan

Day one: Open a Fidelity Roth IRA. Deposit $5. Buy $5 of FZROX. Set up a $25 automatic monthly contribution. Write down the date. You are now an investor. The $5 will probably be worth $40 in thirty years at average market returns. The habit of depositing monthly will make you rich. The compound interest on the habit — not just the money — is the point.

When you get a raise, increase the contribution. When you pay off a credit card, redirect that payment to the IRA. The system is the same at $25/month as it is at $500/month. You're just running it at a different speed. The direction is right. Start now with whatever you have.
